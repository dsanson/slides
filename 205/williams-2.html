T-Sentences

T-sentences are sentences of the form:

T1
“Snow is white” is true if and only if snow is white.

On the left, we mention a sentence and ascribe truth to it. On the right, we use that same sentence. Thw LHS ia about a sentence, a piece of language. The RHS is about snow, stuff in the world.

T-Sentences as a Constraint

A plausible constraint on any theory of truth:

  • “Any adequate account [of truth] must in some sense explain the correctness of [T-]sentences.” (64)

Correspondence Theory of Truth

Correspondence Theory
Truth is a correspondence relation between a sentence (a linguistic item) and a fact (a worldly item).
  1. Each T-sentence expresses this relation. The RHS is the sentence; the LHS is the fact.

Trouble for CT

T2
“Lucy is in the kitchen or Lucy is in the garden” is true if and only Lucy is in the kitchen or Lucy is in the garden.
T3
“Someone is in the garden” is true if and only if someone is in the garden.
T4
“Nobody is in the garden” is true if and only if nobody is in the garden.

Trouble for CT

There is no account of facts that at once is general enough for the purpose and does more than trivially reiterate the content of the sentences for which it is supposed to be illuiminating the truth-conditions. That is to say, there can be no interesting correspondence theory. (65)

Minimalism

Minimalist or Disquotational Theory
Truth is merely a device for disquotation—a way to assert something that we cannot express directly.
  1. Snow is white.
  2. “Snow is white” is three words long.
  3. “Snow is white” is true.
  1. and (4) both look like they are about language, in contrast to (2), which is about the world. But the minimalist says that (4) is actually just another way of saying (2), and so, like (2), is just about the world. Generally, quotation is a device for shifting from using words to mentioning them. Truth is a device for disquotation, for shifting back to using the quoted words rather than mentioning them.

Minimalism and Indirect Assertion

Suppose Jones told you something, and I trust Jones, but I don’t know what he said. I might say,

  1. What Jones said was true.

Suppose Jones said,

  1. The power is out because a storm knocked down the power lines.

For the disquotationalist, saying (6), in this context, just is a way of saying (7). And, given the situation, this is useful, because I cannot say (7) directly (since I don’t know what Jones said).

Truth and “the Value of Truth”

Suppose Minimalism is correct. We might argue:

  1. Truth in itself comes to nothing.
  2. So the value of truth in itself comes to nothing.
  3. So whatever value truth has it must get from something else.

See Williams’s objeciton, p. 65-66.

Assertions and Truth

  1. Assertions aim at truth.
  2. Truth is the norm of assertion.
  3. Assertions are supposed to be/expected to be true.

What exactly is the connection here?

The Analogy with Belief

Beliefs also can be said to aim at the truth, to be supposed to be true, to be subject to norms of truth. It is an objection to a belief that it is false. In fact, in the case of a belief, it is a fatal objection, in the sense that if the person who has the belief accepts the objec tion, he thereby ceases to have the belief, or at least it retreats to the subconscious: if a person recognizes that the content of his belief is false, in virtue of this alone he abandons his belief in it. But there is no comparable way in which falsehood is a fatal objection to an assertion. (67)

Act/object ambiguity

Act/object ambiguity
‘assertion’ can refer to what someone asserts (the content of their assertion) or to the act of asserting. (Other words that share this ambiguity: ‘desire’, ‘belief’, ‘observation’.)

Is the claim that truth is the norm of assertion a claim about the act or the content of assertion? (Other norms that govern the act: don’t be rude or tactless; don’t reveal secrets.)

Maybe falsehood is just an objection to content? See the argument against this across p. 67-68.
